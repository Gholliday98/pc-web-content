CNC routing and CNC milling are both computer-controlled cutting processes, and for buyers unfamiliar with the distinction, the difference can seem like a technicality. CNC milling brings the rigidity, fixturing precision, and tool control of a machining center to plastic fabrication, producing parts with tighter tolerances and more complex three-dimensional geometry than a router can reliably achieve. What Is the Difference Between CNC Milling and CNC Routing for Plastic?

CNC routing and CNC milling both use rotating cutting tools on computer-controlled axes, but the machine platforms are fundamentally different in their rigidity, spindle characteristics, and fixturing capability. A CNC router is a large-format, relatively high-speed machine optimized for profiling and cutting sheet material across a wide working area. It excels at two-dimensional profiles, panel cutting, and shallow pocketing in sheet goods. Its frame and gantry design prioritizes working area over rigidity, which limits the cutting forces it can apply and the tolerances it can hold, particularly on deep pockets, close-tolerance bores, and features with tight positional relationships.

A CNC machining center (commonly called a CNC mill) is a rigid, heavily constructed machine with a precision spindle, mechanical workholding, and a compact working envelope optimized for dimensional accuracy rather than sheet area. It can apply significantly higher cutting forces, hold tighter tolerances on three-dimensional features, and produce precision bores, slots, pockets, and surface finishes that are beyond the reliable capability of router platforms. For plastic parts that require tolerances tighter than ±0.005 inch, complex three-dimensional geometry machined from bar or plate stock, or features with precise positional relationships to each other, CNC milling on a machining center is the correct process.

The practical decision rule is straightforward: if the part can be profiled or pocketed from sheet stock at tolerances achievable by a router (±0.005 to ±0.015 inch), routing is the more efficient process. If the part requires tighter tolerances, complex geometry machined from solid bar or thick plate, precision bores, or three-dimensional features that require multiple setups with precise positional control, CNC milling is the correct specification. Many plastic fabrication jobs require both — a router-cut blank that is then milled to final geometry and tolerance.

What Types of Plastic Parts Are Best Suited to CNC Milling?

CNC milling at Plastic-Craft Products is applied to plastic parts where dimensional precision, complex geometry, or tight feature relationships are the governing requirements. The part types that most consistently call for milling rather than routing are:

  • Precision housings and enclosures: Machined from solid plate or bar stock with tight-tolerance bores, precisely located mounting holes, and flat reference surfaces. Common in electronic assemblies, optical instruments, and industrial equipment where the housing geometry determines the alignment of components installed within it.
  • Fluid manifolds and flow components: Plastic manifolds in acrylic, Delrin, PEEK, or polypropylene machined with precision internal passage geometry, port locations, and face sealing surfaces. Used in chemical processing, laboratory equipment, medical devices, and semiconductor process systems.
  • Jigs and fixtures: Precision tooling plates and workholding fixtures in phenolic, HDPE, or Delrin machined to hold tight positional tolerances. Dimensional errors in a fixture propagate directly into every part made with it.
  • Precision bearing and bushing components: Nylon, UHMW, PEEK, and Torlon bearing components machined to precise bore diameters and outer diameters with surface finish requirements that determine bearing performance.
  • Test and inspection fixtures: Precision plastic plates and blocks used as go/no-go gauges, inspection fixtures, or dimensional reference standards where the plastic component's own dimensional accuracy is the functional requirement of the part.
  • Multi-feature structural components: Parts machined from thick plate with multiple faces, pockets, bores, and slots that must maintain precise positional and angular relationships to each other across multiple setups.

What Tolerances Does CNC Milling Achieve in Plastic?

CNC milling of engineering plastics on a rigid machining center achieves tolerances significantly tighter than those available from router platforms. For most rigid engineering plastics — Delrin, nylon, phenolic, acrylic, polycarbonate, and PEEK — profile tolerances of ±0.002 to ±0.005 inch are routinely achievable, and bore tolerances of ±0.001 to ±0.002 inch are practical for press and slip fits in precision assemblies. Surface flatness on milled faces can be held to 0.001 to 0.003 inch over the feature length for rigid materials, which is necessary for leak-free sealing surfaces on fluid manifolds and optical component mounting plates.

The tolerance achievable in a given material depends primarily on its stiffness, thermal expansion coefficient, and tendency to deflect or spring back under cutting forces. Delrin is one of the most dimensionally stable engineering plastics and holds tight tolerances reliably in milling. PEEK and phenolic are similarly well-behaved. Softer, more flexible materials like UHMW, polypropylene, and HDPE require more careful process management to achieve tolerances comparable to rigid materials, typically ±0.003 to ±0.005 inch rather than the sub-thousandth range possible in Delrin or PEEK.

Thermal expansion is a factor that must be accounted for in tight-tolerance plastic milling. Plastics have significantly higher coefficients of thermal expansion than metals, and the heat generated during milling can cause parts to change dimension measurably between the cut and the inspection. For tight-tolerance work in plastics, allowing parts to stabilize to ambient temperature before final inspection is essential, and tolerances tighter than ±0.001 inch generally require careful temperature management throughout the machining and inspection process.

What Materials Are Compatible with CNC Milling and Which Require Special Handling?

CNC milling is compatible with virtually the entire range of engineering thermoplastics and thermosets. For aerospace, defense, and regulated programs where full material traceability and process documentation are required, Plastic-Craft Products holds ISO 9001:2015 and AS9100D certifications covering all CNC milling operations — meaning the quality management system, dimensional inspection records, and material certifications for a milled part all originate from a single AS9100D-certified source. This matters for prime contractors and government programs that require their supply chain to operate under approved quality systems at every tier.

Rigid engineering plastics including Delrin, nylon, PEEK, Ultem, phenolic, and acrylic machine cleanly with standard carbide tooling and produce good surface finishes and consistent tolerances. Polycarbonate requires care around stress cracking at sharp internal corners and benefits from generous radii in pocket geometry. PTFE is soft and tends to deform under clamping pressure, requiring light fixturing and careful tool selection to prevent movement during cutting.

High-performance materials including Torlon and Vespel require more specialized tooling and process management due to their extreme hardness and abrasiveness compared to standard engineering plastics. These materials may also require post-machining thermal treatment to relieve cutting stresses and stabilize final dimensions. Plastic-Craft Products machines these materials as part of their standard capability and can advise on post-machining treatment requirements for specific part geometries and tolerance specifications.

What Should You Specify When Ordering CNC Milled Plastic Parts?

A complete specification for CNC milled plastic parts begins with a fully dimensioned drawing or CAD model that identifies the part geometry, all critical dimensions with tolerances, surface finish requirements on key faces, and the material specification including grade and any certifications required. For precision bearing and bushing components, specifying the fit class (H7/g6 for running fits, H7/p6 for press fits, etc.) rather than bilateral tolerances alone allows the machinist to apply the appropriate process to achieve the functional fit rather than merely the dimensional target.

Surface finish specification is often overlooked on plastic part drawings but matters significantly for sealing surfaces, sliding contact surfaces, and optical applications. Specifying Ra values on critical surfaces — typically 32 to 63 microinch Ra for general machined surfaces, 16 to 32 microinch Ra for sealing faces, and 8 microinch Ra or better for bearing bores — allows the machinist to select the appropriate finish pass parameters and verify the result. Plastic-Craft Products provides first-article dimensional inspection and surface finish measurement for precision programs, delivering a measurement report with the first production lot to confirm the part meets the drawing before full production release.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between CNC milling and CNC routing for plastic?

CNC routing uses a large-format gantry machine optimized for sheet profiling and panel cutting, typically holding tolerances of ±0.005 to ±0.015 inch. CNC milling uses a rigid machining center with precision workholding, holding tolerances of ±0.001 to ±0.005 inch on most engineering plastics. Parts requiring tight tolerances, complex three-dimensional geometry from solid stock, or precision bores should be milled rather than routed.

What tolerances can CNC milling hold in Delrin and PEEK?

Delrin and PEEK are among the most dimensionally stable engineering plastics for milling. Profile tolerances of ±0.002 to ±0.003 inch and bore tolerances of ±0.001 to ±0.0015 inch are routinely achievable in both materials under controlled conditions. Surface flatness of 0.001 to 0.002 inch over the feature length is practical for sealing faces. Tolerances tighter than ±0.001 inch require careful thermal management during machining and inspection.

Can plastic manifolds with internal passages be CNC milled?

Yes. Plastic manifolds with internal fluid passages are a common CNC milling application at Plastic-Craft Products. The passages are milled as interconnected bores and channels from multiple faces of the block, with face sealing surfaces milled flat for O-ring or gasket sealing. Common manifold materials include acrylic (for visible flow inspection), Delrin, polypropylene, and PEEK depending on the chemical and temperature environment.

Why does thermal expansion matter for tight-tolerance plastic milling?

Plastics expand and contract significantly more than metals with temperature changes. Heat generated during milling can cause a plastic part to measure larger during cutting than it will after it cools to ambient temperature, leading to undersized parts if the effect is not accounted for. For tolerances tighter than ±0.002 inch, parts must be allowed to stabilize to ambient temperature before final inspection.
