What Is Vacuum Forming and How Does the Process Work?

Vacuum forming is a thermoforming process in which a flat thermoplastic sheet is clamped in a frame, heated until it reaches its forming temperature, and then drawn down over or into a mold by applying vacuum pressure beneath the softened sheet. Atmospheric pressure forces the heated plastic to conform precisely to the mold surface, after which the sheet cools and retains the three-dimensional shape of the tool. The formed part is then trimmed to its final dimensions by CNC routing, die cutting, or hand trimming depending on the part geometry and production volume.

Heat forming is closely related but uses direct mechanical pressure, matched tooling, or a combination of heat and forming fixtures rather than vacuum alone. Both processes share the same fundamental characteristic: they start with flat sheet stock and produce three-dimensional geometry without the high tooling cost and lead time of injection molding. For many enclosure, cover, tray, and housing applications, vacuum and heat forming delivers production-intent plastic components at a fraction of the investment required for injection-molded equivalents.

The process is limited to geometries achievable from sheet stock — open-faced shapes, relatively shallow draws relative to part width, and designs without enclosed volumes or true undercuts. Within those constraints, vacuum forming offers exceptional design flexibility, rapid tooling, and the ability to form virtually any thermoplastic available in sheet form, including acrylic, ABS, polycarbonate, PETG, HDPE, polypropylene, and PVC.

What Are the Advantages of Vacuum Forming Over Injection Molding?

Vacuum forming delivers three advantages over injection molding that make it the correct process for a defined range of production applications. The first is tooling cost: vacuum form tooling in aluminum or composite can be produced for a fraction of the cost of an injection mold, often by an order of magnitude or more for large parts. A vacuum form tool for a medium-sized equipment enclosure might cost $3,000 to $15,000; the injection mold equivalent, if it were even physically feasible at the same size, would cost many times that. For programs with moderate annual volumes and parts too large for economical injection molding, vacuum forming changes the economics of custom plastic components entirely.

The second advantage is tooling lead time. Vacuum form tooling can be designed, machined, and ready for first articles in one to three weeks for most part geometries. Injection mold tooling typically requires six to sixteen weeks from design approval to first production samples, with additional time for sampling iterations and corrections. For programs where time to market or field prototype availability is a constraint, vacuum forming's compressed tooling cycle is a genuine competitive advantage.

The third advantage is material flexibility. Vacuum forming can process virtually any thermoplastic available in sheet form — including materials that are difficult or unavailable in injection-moldable grades, or where the specific properties of a stocked sheet grade are required in the finished part. This flexibility allows engineers to specify the exact material grade, color, texture, and thickness needed rather than being constrained to what is available in injection molding pellet form.

What Plastics Can Be Vacuum Formed and What Determines Material Selection?

All amorphous thermoplastics and most semi-crystalline thermoplastics can be vacuum formed from sheet stock. The most commonly formed materials in industrial and commercial applications are ABS, acrylic, polycarbonate, PETG, HDPE, polypropylene, and PVC. Each material has its own optimal forming temperature range, draw ratio capability, and post-forming dimensional stability, which the forming process parameters must be tuned to match.

  • ABS: The most widely vacuum-formed engineering plastic. Excellent formability, good impact resistance, paintable, and available in a wide range of colors and textures. The standard material for equipment enclosures, automotive interior components, and consumer product housings.
  • Acrylic: Forms well at elevated temperatures and retains optical clarity in the formed part. Used for display domes, lenses, light diffusers, and architectural glazing components where transparency and surface quality are required in a three-dimensional form.
  • Polycarbonate: Requires higher forming temperatures than ABS or acrylic and must be dried before forming to prevent surface defects. Used where impact resistance in a formed part is required alongside moderate transparency.
  • PETG: Excellent formability at lower temperatures than acrylic or polycarbonate, retains clarity, and produces lower internal stress in formed parts. Preferred for food contact trays, medical packaging, and display components where lower forming temperature is advantageous.
  • HDPE and polypropylene: Semi-crystalline materials that require more precise temperature control than amorphous plastics. Used for chemical-resistant trays, automotive underhood components, and applications requiring chemical resistance in a formed geometry.
  • PVC: Good formability and widely used for trays, blisters, and protective covers. Note that PVC cannot be laser cut after forming — CNC routing is the correct trimming process for all formed PVC components.

What Should You Provide When Requesting a Vacuum Forming Quote?

For buyers requesting a vacuum forming quote, the most useful information to provide upfront is part geometry (a drawing, CAD file, or physical sample), the material specification including type, grade, color, and thickness, the required production quantity both initially and annually, and the required dimensional tolerances for the finished formed part. If an existing part is being replaced or replicated, a physical sample is often the most efficient starting point for tooling design. If the part is new, a CAD file or dimensioned sketch is sufficient for a budgetary quote and tooling design initiation.

Draft angles are a critical design consideration for vacuum-formed parts that buyers new to the process sometimes overlook. The mold geometry must include sufficient draft — typically a minimum of 3 to 5 degrees on vertical walls — to allow the formed part to release from the tool cleanly without distortion. Parts designed without vacuum forming in mind may require modifications before tooling can be produced. Plastic-Craft Products can advise on design for formability as part of the quoting process, identifying any geometry changes needed to optimize the part for vacuum forming before tooling investment is committed.

How thick can vacuum formed plastic be?

Vacuum forming can process sheet thicknesses ranging from approximately 0.030 inch up to 0.500 inch and beyond depending on the material and part geometry. Thicker sheet requires longer heating cycles and produces more robust formed parts, but also requires more forming pressure and produces more wall thinning at corners and deep draws. The correct sheet thickness is determined by the structural requirements of the finished part, not the forming process alone.

What tolerances can vacuum forming hold?

Vacuum forming holds looser tolerances than CNC machining — typically ±0.020 to ±0.060 inch on formed dimensions depending on part size, material, and feature location. Tolerances on trimmed edges after CNC routing are tighter, typically ±0.005 to ±0.010 inch. For applications requiring tighter tolerances on critical features, secondary machining operations can be performed on the formed part after trimming.

Can vacuum formed parts be trimmed and finished after forming?

Yes. Vacuum formed parts are routinely trimmed by CNC routing, drilled, tapped, painted, screen-printed, or given additional surface finishes after forming.
