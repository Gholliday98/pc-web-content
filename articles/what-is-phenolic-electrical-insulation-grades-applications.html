Phenolic laminate is one of the oldest engineered plastics still in active industrial use — and its longevity is no accident. G-10, Garolite, CE, LE, and other phenolic grades combine high mechanical strength, outstanding electrical insulation, and dimensional stability that thermoplastics cannot replicate at elevated temperatures. For electrical engineers, equipment designers, and procurement specialists specifying insulating substrates and structural components, understanding the differences between phenolic grades is the foundation of a correct specification. This guide covers the material, the grades, and how to order it right.

What Is Phenolic Laminate and How Is It Made?

Phenolic laminate is a thermoset composite material produced by impregnating layers of reinforcing substrate — woven glass fabric, paper, or canvas — with a thermosetting resin, typically phenol-formaldehyde or epoxy, and then curing the stack under heat and pressure. The result is a rigid, dense sheet or rod that combines the mechanical strength of the reinforcing fiber with the chemical and electrical properties of the resin matrix. Unlike thermoplastics, phenolic laminates are thermoset materials that do not melt or soften when reheated — they maintain their structural integrity up to their continuous service temperature limits.

The combination of reinforcement type and resin system determines the grade designation and the resulting property profile. Different reinforcements — glass fabric, cotton canvas, linen fabric, or paper — produce significantly different mechanical, electrical, and moisture resistance characteristics. The NEMA grade system standardizes these combinations and is the primary reference for specifying phenolic laminates in electrical and industrial applications. What Are the Key Phenolic Grades and When Should Each Be Specified?

Selecting the correct phenolic grade is the most consequential decision in the specification process. Each grade is optimized for a different combination of electrical, mechanical, and environmental requirements, and substituting one for another can result in premature failure in electrical insulation applications or substandard mechanical performance in structural ones. The primary grades stocked at Plastic-Craft Products are:

  • G-10 / Garolite G-10 (NEMA Grade G-10): Woven fiberglass with epoxy resin. The highest-performance general-purpose grade — excellent electrical insulation, low moisture absorption, and high mechanical strength. Standard specification for PCB substrates, electrical insulating components, and structural applications requiring consistent dielectric properties in wet environments.
  • G-11 (NEMA Grade G-11): Similar to G-10 but with a higher-temperature epoxy resin system. Retains flexural strength and electrical properties at elevated temperatures where G-10 would begin to soften. Specified for motor windings and transformer insulation in high-temperature environments.
  • CE (NEMA Grade CE): Cotton canvas reinforcement with phenolic resin. Lower electrical performance than glass-reinforced grades but significantly better machinability and impact resistance. Standard specification for bushings, gears, jigs, and structural components where machinability and toughness matter more than electrical insulation.
  • LE (NEMA Grade LE): Linen fabric reinforcement with phenolic resin. Finer weave than CE, producing a smoother surface finish and better dimensional consistency in machined parts. Preferred for precision bushings, bearing components, and electrical panels where CE's coarser canvas texture is undesirable.
  • XX / XXX (NEMA Grades XX and XXX): Paper reinforcement with phenolic resin. Lower mechanical strength than fabric-reinforced grades but excellent machinability and moderate electrical insulation. Used in electrical panels, terminal boards, and general-purpose insulating components in dry indoor environments.

What Are the Key Properties of Phenolic Laminate?

Phenolic laminates are specified for applications where thermoplastics are insufficiently rigid, thermally stable, or electrically reliable. High dielectric strength and volume resistivity make them effective electrical insulators across a range of voltages and frequencies. Glass-reinforced grades like G-10 maintain these properties even after moisture exposure, while paper and fabric grades absorb more moisture and are better suited for dry or indoor environments.

Phenolic laminates are dimensionally stable under load and temperature in ways that most thermoplastics are not. They do not creep significantly under sustained compressive or flexural load, making them reliable in applications where tight clearances must be maintained over long service periods. Continuous service temperatures range from approximately 250°F (120°C) for standard phenolic grades to over 300°F (150°C) for glass-epoxy grades like G-10 and G-11. This thermal stability, combined with flame resistance in most grades, makes phenolic laminate a standard material in electrical switchgear, transformer assemblies, and motor components.

Machinability varies significantly by grade. Canvas and linen grades (CE, LE) machine freely with standard tooling and produce clean edges. G-10 and other glass-reinforced grades are significantly more abrasive and require carbide tooling and appropriate dust management. All grades produce fine particulate dust that requires respiratory protection during machining operations.

What Is Phenolic Laminate Used For?

Phenolic laminate's combination of electrical insulation, mechanical strength, and thermal stability makes it a foundational material across electrical engineering and precision manufacturing. It is specified in applications where the operating environment — high voltage, elevated temperature, or sustained mechanical load — would cause thermoplastics to fail or deform. For aerospace and defense programs requiring documented material traceability, Plastic-Craft Products holds ISO 9001:2015 and AS9100D certifications, providing the quality documentation chain that regulated industries and flight programs require when sourcing electrical insulation and structural components.

  • Electrical switchgear and panels: Insulating barriers, terminal boards, and bus bar supports in high-voltage switchgear where dielectric strength and arc resistance are critical.
  • Motor and transformer insulation: Slot liners, end turn supports, coil spacers, and winding mandrels where thermal stability and dielectric reliability under continuous duty are required.
  • Printed circuit board substrates: G-10 is the base material for FR-4 PCB substrates — the industry-standard substrate for rigid printed circuit boards across electronics manufacturing.
  • Structural and tooling components: Jigs, fixtures, test probes, and tooling plates where dimensional stability, hardness, and freedom from metallic contamination are required.
  • Bushings and wear components: CE and LE grade phenolic rod is used for bushings, bearings, and wear components in machinery where the self-lubricating properties of canvas or linen reinforcement reduce friction against metal shafts.
  • Defense and aerospace insulation: Radar equipment housings, avionics insulating substrates, and structural components where electrical isolation, mechanical strength, and dimensional stability under thermal cycling are simultaneously required.

What Should You Specify When Ordering Phenolic?

Grade selection is the first and most critical specification decision. G-10 is the correct choice for any application requiring both electrical insulation and moisture resistance. CE or LE is correct when machinability and impact resistance are the primary requirements and electrical performance demands are moderate. XX or XXX paper grades are appropriate for low-cost, low-stress electrical insulating applications in dry indoor environments. Mixing grades in an assembly without confirming compatibility of electrical and mechanical properties is a common source of field failures.

For machined components, buyers should specify finished dimensions with required tolerances, surface finish needed on machined faces, and whether any specific NEMA grade certification is required. Phenolic rod is available in a range of diameters for turned components such as bushings and spacers. Sheet is available in standard and custom thicknesses for cut, routed, or drilled flat components. Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between G-10 and Garolite?

Garolite is a trade name used by Laird Performance Materials for their line of phenolic and epoxy laminates. Garolite G-10 refers to the same NEMA G-10 grade — woven fiberglass with epoxy resin — as any other G-10 product. The material properties and grade designation are defined by the NEMA standard, not the brand name.

What is the difference between G-10 and FR-4?

G-10 and FR-4 are both woven fiberglass epoxy laminates with nearly identical mechanical and electrical properties. FR-4 includes a flame-retardant additive meeting UL 94 V-0 requirements, while standard G-10 does not. For most structural and electrical insulation applications outside electronics manufacturing, G-10 and FR-4 are interchangeable. For PCB applications where UL flammability rating is required, FR-4 is the correct specification.

Is phenolic laminate safe to machine?

Phenolic laminate is not electrically hazardous to machine, but the dust produced — particularly from glass-reinforced grades like G-10 — is a respiratory and skin irritant. Machining should be performed with appropriate respiratory protection, dust collection, and skin protection. Canvas and paper grades produce less abrasive dust but still require proper ventilation and PPE.

Can phenolic laminate be used outdoors?

Glass-reinforced grades like G-10 perform well in wet environments due to low moisture absorption, but phenolic laminates are generally not recommended for prolonged direct UV exposure without protective coatings. Paper and canvas grades absorb moisture and should not be used outdoors. For outdoor electrical insulation, G-10 or G-11 with an appropriate surface treatment is the correct specification.
