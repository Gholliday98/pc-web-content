There is a short list of materials that engineers turn to when the chemical environment would destroy everything else — and PTFE sits at the top of it. Polytetrafluoroethylene, sold under the trade name Teflon, is virtually inert to every known industrial chemical, operates continuously at temperatures up to 500°F, and has the lowest coefficient of friction of any solid material. This guide covers what PTFE is, where it genuinely earns its place in a specification, and what to know before ordering it.

What Is PTFE and What Makes It Chemically Inert?

PTFE stands for polytetrafluoroethylene, a fluoropolymer produced by polymerizing tetrafluoroethylene monomer. It was discovered accidentally by DuPont chemist Roy Plunkett in 1938 and commercialized under the Teflon trade name. The material's extraordinary chemical inertness comes from its molecular structure: the carbon-fluorine bonds in PTFE are among the strongest in organic chemistry, and the fluorine atoms form a continuous protective sheath around the carbon backbone that is effectively impenetrable to almost all reactive chemicals.

The result is a material that resists concentrated acids, alkalis, organic solvents, oxidizing agents, and virtually every other chemical that would attack or degrade competing plastics, metals, or elastomers. The only known substances that affect PTFE under normal conditions are molten alkali metals and certain fluorinating agents — making it the specification of last resort in chemical processing environments where no other plastic will survive. What Are the Key Properties of PTFE Sheet and Rod?

PTFE's adoption across chemical processing, semiconductor manufacturing, and high-temperature applications is driven by a performance profile unlike any other engineering thermoplastic. The properties that most influence specification decisions are:

  • Chemical resistance: Resistant to virtually all industrial chemicals, solvents, acids, alkalis, and oxidizing agents across the full pH range. No other standard engineering plastic matches PTFE's breadth of chemical inertness.
  • Temperature range: Continuous service from -328°F to +500°F (-200°C to +260°C) — the widest operating temperature range of any standard engineering thermoplastic, maintaining flexibility even at cryogenic temperatures.
  • Coefficient of friction: The lowest of any solid material — approximately 0.05 to 0.10 static — making PTFE the standard specification for bearing surfaces, slide plates, and low-friction liners where no lubrication is possible.
  • Non-stick surface: Nothing bonds to PTFE under normal conditions — both its most famous property and a genuine challenge when bonding or sealing PTFE components in assemblies.
  • Electrical insulation: Exceptional dielectric properties across a wide frequency range, making PTFE the preferred insulation material for high-frequency and microwave applications.
  • Biocompatibility: Non-toxic, non-reactive, and approved for use in implantable medical devices and food contact applications where chemical purity is mandatory.
  • Flame resistance: Non-flammable and self-extinguishing — PTFE will not sustain combustion under normal atmospheric conditions.

PTFE's significant limitations are equally important to understand. It has low stiffness and compressive strength relative to other engineering plastics, poor creep resistance under sustained load, and cannot be melt-processed like standard thermoplastics. It is also more expensive than nylon, Delrin, or UHMW. PTFE is correctly specified only when its unique combination of chemical, thermal, and friction properties is genuinely required.

What Is PTFE Used For in Industrial and Precision Applications?

PTFE is specified wherever the combination of extreme chemical resistance, high-temperature performance, and non-stick surface properties simultaneously exceeds what any other plastic can deliver. Its application range is dominated by chemical processing, semiconductor manufacturing, medical devices, and electrical insulation.

  • Chemical processing equipment: Valve seats, pump liners, gaskets, seals, and pipe liners in environments involving concentrated acids, caustics, and solvents that attack all other plastics and most metals.
  • Semiconductor manufacturing: Wafer carriers, wet bench components, chemical distribution liners, and process equipment parts where ultra-high purity and resistance to aggressive cleaning chemicals are mandatory.
  • Electrical and microwave insulation: Cable insulation, circuit board substrates, and high-frequency antenna components where stable dielectric properties across a wide temperature and frequency range are required.
  • Slide bearings and wear pads: Structural slide bearings in bridges and buildings, conveyor wear pads, and low-friction liners where zero lubrication and extreme durability are both required.
  • Medical and pharmaceutical: Implantable device components, surgical instrument liners, and pharmaceutical process equipment where biocompatibility, chemical purity, and sterilization compatibility are required.
  • Food processing: Non-stick liners, conveyor components, and processing surfaces where FDA compliance, cleanability, and resistance to cleaning chemicals are all required simultaneously.

What PTFE Grades Are Available and When Should Each Be Specified?

Unfilled virgin PTFE is the standard specification for most chemical resistance and electrical insulation applications. It offers the full breadth of PTFE's chemical and thermal properties but has relatively low mechanical strength and poor creep resistance under sustained compressive load. For applications requiring better dimensional stability, higher compressive strength, or improved wear resistance under load, filled PTFE grades are available.

Glass-filled PTFE significantly improves compressive strength and creep resistance while retaining most of the chemical resistance of virgin material — the exception being hydrofluoric acid and strong alkalis, which attack the glass fibers. Carbon-filled PTFE improves stiffness, thermal conductivity, and wear resistance, making it the preferred specification for bearing and wear pad applications. Bronze-filled PTFE is used in high-load, high-speed bearing applications where the thermal conductivity of the bronze filler helps dissipate friction heat. When specifying filled PTFE grades, confirming chemical compatibility of both the PTFE matrix and the filler material for the specific environment is essential.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is PTFE the same as Teflon?

Yes. Teflon is Chemours' (formerly DuPont's) trade name for polytetrafluoroethylene. PTFE is the generic chemical name for the same material. Both refer to the same fluoropolymer with the same chemical resistance, temperature rating, and non-stick properties. Other trade names for PTFE include Fluon and Polyflon.

What chemicals is PTFE not resistant to?

PTFE resists virtually all industrial chemicals. The known exceptions under normal conditions are molten alkali metals (sodium, potassium), elemental fluorine at high temperatures, and certain fluorinating agents such as chlorine trifluoride and oxygen difluoride. For almost all other chemical environments, PTFE is the correct specification when no other material will suffice.

Can PTFE be bonded or welded?

Standard adhesives will not bond to PTFE due to its non-stick surface. Bonding requires either mechanical fastening, surface etching with sodium naphthalene solution, or specialized PTFE-compatible adhesive systems. PTFE can be joined by compression fitting, PTFE-lined flanges, or threaded connections in most process applications.

How does PTFE compare to UHMW for low-friction applications?

PTFE has a lower coefficient of friction than UHMW and is the correct specification when chemical resistance or high-temperature performance is also required. UHMW offers significantly better abrasion resistance and compressive strength at a much lower cost, making it the preferred choice for wear pads, chute liners, and conveyor components where chemical exposure is limited and temperature is moderate.
