Ultem sits in a rarified tier of engineering thermoplastics — materials where the performance demands of the application have outpaced what standard engineering plastics can deliver, but where the cost and complexity of PEEK cannot be justified. Inherently flame-retardant, rated for continuous service at 340°F, and transparent amber in its natural form, Ultem (PEI) earns its place in aerospace cabins, medical sterilization equipment, and precision electronic components. This guide covers what Ultem is, where it genuinely belongs in a specification, and how it compares to PEEK and other high-performance alternatives.

What Is Ultem and What Is It Made Of?

Ultem is SABIC's trade name for polyetherimide (PEI), an amorphous thermoplastic produced from the condensation of aromatic dianhydride and aromatic diamine monomers. Its amorphous molecular structure gives it excellent dimensional stability, high strength across a broad temperature range, and an inherent amber transparency that is visually distinctive and practically useful for applications where material identification matters. Ultem was first commercialized in the early 1980s and has since become a standard specification in aerospace interior components, medical devices, and high-frequency electronics.

PEI is available in several grades. Unfilled natural Ultem 1010 is the standard general-purpose grade with the broadest chemical and sterilization compatibility, including FDA compliance for food contact and USP Class VI compliance for medical applications. Ultem 2300 is glass-fiber-reinforced, offering substantially higher stiffness, improved dimensional stability under load, and better creep resistance than the unfilled grade. What Are the Key Properties of Ultem That Drive Its Specification?

Ultem's adoption in demanding applications comes from a combination of properties that places it firmly between standard engineering plastics and the premium performance tier occupied by PEEK. For engineers evaluating whether Ultem is the right specification, the relevant properties are:

  • Continuous service temperature: Rated for continuous use at up to 340°F (170°C) — significantly higher than nylon, Delrin, or polycarbonate, and adequate for the majority of elevated-temperature applications that do not require PEEK's 480°F ceiling.
  • Inherent flame retardancy: Achieves UL 94 V-0 flame rating without additives, with a limiting oxygen index of 47% and extremely low smoke and toxic gas emission — properties that make it the standard choice for aircraft cabin interior components under FAR 25.853 requirements.
  • Mechanical strength: High tensile and flexural strength retained across its full operating temperature range, with excellent creep resistance under sustained load — particularly in the glass-filled 2300 grade.
  • Dimensional stability: Very low moisture absorption and excellent resistance to creep make Ultem reliable for precision components where dimensional consistency must be maintained across varying environments.
  • Biocompatibility: Ultem 1010 meets USP Class VI and ISO 10993 biocompatibility standards and withstands steam autoclave sterilization, gamma irradiation, and EtO sterilization without significant degradation.
  • Dielectric properties: Stable electrical insulation across a wide temperature and frequency range, making Ultem a standard substrate and insulating component material in high-frequency and microwave electronics.
  • Machinability: Machines cleanly to tight tolerances with standard carbide tooling, producing smooth surface finishes suitable for sealing interfaces, precision instrument components, and structural brackets.

Ultem's primary limitations are its susceptibility to strong alkalis and partial solvents such as methylene chloride, and its amorphous structure gives it lower chemical resistance than semi-crystalline PEEK in aggressive solvent environments. For the majority of aerospace, medical, and electronics applications, these limitations do not affect the specification.

What Industries and Applications Specify Ultem?

Ultem is specified across industries where the combination of flame performance, elevated temperature rating, and either biocompatibility or electrical insulation requirements simultaneously disqualifies lower-cost engineering plastics.

  • Aerospace interior components: Overhead bin latches, seat components, lighting bezels, ducting, and structural brackets where FAR 25.853 flame, smoke, and toxicity compliance is mandated.
  • Medical devices and sterilizable equipment: Surgical instrument handles, sterilization trays, reusable device housings, and laboratory equipment where USP Class VI compliance and autoclave resistance are required.
  • Electronics and electrical insulation: Burn-in test sockets, circuit board supports, connector housings, and microwave waveguide components where dimensional stability, dielectric performance, and high operating temperatures coexist.
  • Semiconductor manufacturing: Wafer handling components and process equipment parts requiring high-purity, elevated-temperature performance and resistance to aggressive cleaning protocols.
  • Defense and military equipment: Structural components, instrument housings, and insulating parts in defense systems where flame compliance, dimensional stability, and sustained performance at elevated temperatures are simultaneously required.

How Does Ultem Compare to PEEK?

Ultem and PEEK occupy adjacent tiers in the high-performance thermoplastic hierarchy and are frequently evaluated side by side. PEEK offers a higher continuous service temperature (480°F vs. 340°F), significantly better chemical resistance — particularly against organic solvents — and superior fatigue performance under dynamic loading. PEEK is the correct specification when the application pushes the thermal or chemical limits that Ultem cannot meet.

Ultem is the correct specification when PEEK's performance premium is not required. Ultem costs meaningfully less than PEEK, machines comparably well, and meets the flame, smoke, and toxicity requirements of aerospace cabin applications. For medical applications requiring biocompatibility and sterilization resistance, Ultem 1010 meets the relevant standards at a price point that makes it the standard specification for reusable instrument components where PEEK would be over-specified. The selection between the two comes down to whether the application's thermal and chemical demands genuinely require PEEK's additional performance, or whether Ultem's profile is sufficient.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between Ultem and PEI?

Ultem is SABIC's trade name for polyetherimide (PEI). The two terms refer to the same material — PEI is the generic polymer name, Ultem is the brand name. The material properties are defined by the polymer chemistry, not the brand designation. Other suppliers may sell PEI under different trade names, but the performance characteristics of the base polymer are consistent.

Is Ultem safe for medical and food contact applications?

Yes — Ultem 1010 meets USP Class VI and ISO 10993 biocompatibility standards and is FDA-compliant for food contact. It withstands steam autoclave sterilization, gamma irradiation, and EtO sterilization without significant degradation, making it the standard specification for reusable medical instrument components and sterilization equipment.

Does Ultem meet aerospace flammability requirements?

Yes. Ultem achieves a UL 94 V-0 flame rating inherently — without flame-retardant additives — and meets the FAR 25.853 flame, smoke, and toxicity requirements mandated for aircraft cabin interior components. Its low smoke emission and low toxic gas generation during combustion are critical properties for occupied aerospace environments.

What temperature can Ultem handle continuously?

Ultem has a continuous service temperature of approximately 340°F (170°C) for the unfilled 1010 grade, with the glass-filled 2300 grade offering comparable thermal performance with improved stiffness and creep resistance. For applications requiring continuous service above 340°F, PEEK is the appropriate specification.
