UHMW and nylon are the two most commonly specified plastics for wear, sliding, and friction-reduction applications — and they are specified for good reason in their respective best-fit environments. The problem is that they are also frequently substituted for each other without sufficient understanding of where each material's performance advantage ends and the other's begins. This guide provides the side-by-side comparison that engineers and procurement managers need to make the right call the first time.

What Makes UHMW and Nylon the Go-To Wear Plastics?

UHMW (ultra-high molecular weight polyethylene) and nylon (polyamide) both earn their place in wear applications because they offer self-lubricating surfaces, good impact resistance, and wear performance that far exceeds most metals in sliding contact applications — all at weights significantly lower than metal alternatives. They are specified in conveyor components, bushings, wear pads, guide rails, sprockets, gears, and material handling liners across dozens of industries. The decision between them is driven by a specific set of application variables, not by a simple hierarchy of one being better than the other.

UHMW's defining characteristic is its abrasion resistance in sliding wear applications. With the lowest coefficient of friction of any plastic and a molecular weight that gives it exceptional toughness even at cryogenic temperatures, UHMW outperforms nearly everything else when bulk material is sliding across a surface, when a conveyor chain is running along a guide, or when a part needs to survive millions of cycles of low-load sliding contact. Its moisture immunity and FDA compliance for food contact add practical range to its application profile.

Nylon's defining characteristic is its combination of mechanical strength, stiffness, and load-bearing capacity. Where UHMW is soft and flexible under load, nylon is rigid and dimensionally stable. For gears, precision bushings, and structural wear components where the part must hold a tight tolerance and support meaningful compressive or bending loads, nylon delivers performance that UHMW cannot match at the same geometry. The trade-off is nylon's moisture absorption, which requires design consideration in precision applications.

How Do UHMW and Nylon Compare Across the Key Performance Factors?

The specification decision between UHMW and nylon comes down to a clear set of performance variables. Mapping the application requirements against these factors produces the correct material specification in the majority of cases:

  • Abrasion resistance in sliding wear: UHMW wins. In applications where bulk material, chain, or another surface slides across the plastic continuously, UHMW's extremely high molecular weight produces superior wear life over nylon under equivalent conditions.
  • Compressive load capacity: Nylon wins. Nylon is stiffer and has higher compressive strength than UHMW. Under concentrated point loads or in precision bearing applications, UHMW will deform and cold-flow, while nylon holds its shape and maintains its tolerance.
  • Coefficient of friction: UHMW wins. UHMW has the lowest coefficient of friction of any plastic — comparable to PTFE — and requires no external lubrication in most sliding applications. Oil-filled nylon narrows the gap significantly but does not close it entirely.
  • Moisture sensitivity: UHMW wins. UHMW absorbs essentially no moisture and its dimensions and mechanical properties are completely unaffected by wet environments. Nylon absorbs moisture and grows dimensionally, which affects precision fit in tight-tolerance applications.
  • Temperature resistance: Nylon wins. Standard nylon grades handle continuous service to approximately 185–200°F with better stiffness retention at elevated temperatures than UHMW, which begins to soften meaningfully above its service rating.
  • Machinability: Nylon wins for precision. Nylon holds tighter tolerances, produces cleaner edges, and is less prone to deformation during machining than UHMW. UHMW's softness causes it to deflect under cutting forces, making tight tolerances more difficult to achieve consistently.
  • FDA and food contact compliance: UHMW wins. Natural (white) virgin UHMW meets FDA, USDA, and NSF requirements for direct food contact without additives. Nylon's food contact compliance depends on the specific grade and any additives present.
  • Cost: UHMW is typically less expensive than nylon rod and sheet at equivalent dimensions. For high-volume wear liner and sheet applications, UHMW's cost advantage can be meaningful at scale.

When Is UHMW the Right Choice?

UHMW is the correct specification when the primary requirement is surface wear resistance in a sliding, abrading, or bulk-material-contact environment and when dimensional precision under compressive load is not critical. The applications where UHMW consistently earns its specification are chute and hopper liners, conveyor wear strips and guide rails, truck bed and bucket liners, dock fenders and marine wear components, food processing conveyor guides, and any application where a surface must survive continuous abrasive contact with bulk materials, product packaging, or chains.

UHMW's immunity to moisture makes it the unambiguous choice over nylon in any wet, submerged, or high-humidity wear application. A nylon wear strip in a wet food processing environment will absorb moisture, swell dimensionally, and potentially bind in its retaining channel. A UHMW wear strip in the same environment will perform identically to its dry-environment specification. For any wear application in a wet process environment, UHMW is the default specification unless a specific mechanical property of nylon is required.

When Is Nylon the Right Choice?

Nylon is the correct specification when the wear application also involves meaningful structural loads, precision tolerances, or elevated temperature cycling — conditions where UHMW's softness and tendency to cold-flow under sustained compressive load would produce dimensional instability or premature failure. Gears, precision bushings, bearing components, sprockets, cam followers, and structural wear pads that must maintain their geometry under load are all applications where nylon's superior stiffness and compressive strength justify the specification over UHMW.

Oil-filled nylon narrows the friction gap with UHMW significantly and is the correct specification when both low friction and structural performance are required in a dry-running application. For bearing and bushing applications in automated machinery where external lubrication is impractical, oil-filled nylon delivers a practical combination of self-lubrication and load-bearing capability that neither standard nylon nor UHMW alone can match.

What Is the Decision Framework for Choosing Between UHMW and Nylon?

The correct material for a wear application is determined by three questions in sequence. First: is the primary wear mechanism surface abrasion from bulk material, chain, or continuous sliding contact? If yes, specify UHMW unless structural load factors override this. Second: does the component carry meaningful compressive loads, require tight tolerances, or need to maintain precise geometry under sustained stress? If yes, specify nylon or oil-filled nylon. Third: will the component operate in a wet or humid environment? If yes and structural loads are not the primary concern, UHMW's moisture immunity tips the balance in its favor.

The most common specification error in this comparison is using nylon where UHMW would last significantly longer in a high-abrasion, low-load application — driven by nylon's familiarity as an engineering plastic or by the incorrect assumption that nylon's higher stiffness translates to better wear performance. Conversely, using UHMW in a precision gear or bushing application because of its low friction will produce a component that deforms under load and loses its tolerance within its first service interval. Frequently Asked Questions

Which is more abrasion-resistant, UHMW or nylon?

UHMW is more abrasion-resistant than standard nylon in sliding and bulk-material-contact wear applications. UHMW's extremely high molecular weight produces a surface that outlasts nylon in conveyor liners, chute liners, and continuous sliding contact applications. Oil-filled nylon narrows the gap in rotating and oscillating contact applications, but in high-abrasion bulk-material environments UHMW is the stronger specification.

Can UHMW be used for gears and precision bushings?

UHMW is not the correct specification for precision gears or tight-tolerance bushings. Its softness causes cold-flow and dimensional creep under sustained compressive load, which means it will lose its geometry in service. For precision machined wear components that must maintain tolerances under load, nylon or oil-filled nylon is the correct specification.

Does nylon absorb water and does it affect performance?

Yes — nylon absorbs moisture from the environment, which causes dimensional growth and a reduction in stiffness. For precision assemblies this must be accounted for in the design. In wet or high-humidity wear applications where dimensional stability is critical, UHMW's complete moisture immunity gives it a significant practical advantage over nylon.

Is oil-filled nylon better than UHMW for bearings?

For precision bearing and bushing applications under meaningful compressive loads, oil-filled nylon is typically the better specification because it combines low friction with the stiffness and load-bearing capacity that UHMW lacks. For low-load, high-wear sliding applications like conveyor wear strips and chute liners, UHMW remains the better choice regardless of lubrication.
