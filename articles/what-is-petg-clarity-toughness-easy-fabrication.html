Not every application needs the scratch resistance of acrylic or the impact performance of polycarbonate — and for the projects that fall in between, PETG is frequently the most practical answer. Glycol-modified polyethylene terephthalate combines optical clarity, meaningful impact resistance, and an ease of fabrication that makes it accessible in ways that acrylic and polycarbonate are not. This guide covers what PETG is, where it fits in a specification, and how it compares to the materials it most often replaces.

What Is PETG and How Does It Differ from Standard PET?

PETG stands for glycol-modified polyethylene terephthalate, a thermoplastic produced by modifying standard PET resin — the same polymer used in beverage bottles — with a co-monomer during polymerization. The addition of cyclohexanedimethanol (CHDM) in place of some of the standard ethylene glycol disrupts the crystallization of the polymer, producing an amorphous material rather than the semi-crystalline PET used in packaging. This structural change is responsible for PETG's optical clarity, improved impact resistance, and significantly lower forming temperatures compared to standard PET — all of which make it far more practical for sheet fabrication, thermoforming, and CNC machining.

The result is a material that is visually similar to acrylic in its transparency and surface quality, but meaningfully tougher in impact resistance and more forgiving in fabrication. PETG does not shatter or crack the way acrylic can under impact, and it thermoforms at lower temperatures and over a wider processing window than polycarbonate — properties that matter considerably in fabrication shops producing custom enclosures, display components, and formed parts. What Are the Key Properties of PETG Sheet?

PETG's adoption in fabrication, display, and protective applications comes from a property profile that positions it as a practical middle ground between acrylic and polycarbonate. The properties most relevant to specification decisions are:

  • Optical clarity: Transmits approximately 90% of visible light in clear grades, producing transparency comparable to acrylic and suitable for display, glazing, and inspection window applications.
  • Impact resistance: Significantly tougher than acrylic — PETG will bend and deform before fracturing, making it a safer choice in applications where acrylic's brittleness is a concern without requiring the full cost premium of polycarbonate.
  • Thermoformability: Thermoforms at lower temperatures and over a wider forming window than acrylic or polycarbonate, producing lower internal stress in formed parts and easier processing on standard thermoforming equipment.
  • Chemical resistance: Resistant to many dilute acids, alkalis, and aqueous solutions. Better resistance to certain alcohols and cleaning agents than acrylic, though less resistant to aromatic solvents and ketones.
  • Machinability: Machines cleanly with standard tooling and produces good surface finishes. Less prone to chipping at edges than acrylic, and generates less heat during machining than polycarbonate.
  • Bondability: Bonds well with compatible solvent cements and adhesives and can be heat-welded — useful for fabricated enclosures and assemblies where acrylic's more sensitive bonding requirements complicate production.
  • FDA compliance: Meets FDA requirements for food contact in many applications, making it suitable for food service display, food processing equipment covers, and packaging-related fabrication.

PETG's primary limitations compared to acrylic are lower surface hardness — it scratches more easily and edges cannot be flame-polished to optical clarity — and lower UV stability. For long-term outdoor applications, PETG is not the correct specification without UV-stabilized grades or protective coatings.

How Does PETG Compare to Acrylic and Polycarbonate?

The most common specification decision involving PETG is a three-way comparison against acrylic and polycarbonate. Acrylic leads on optical clarity (92% light transmission vs. 90% for PETG), surface hardness, and scratch resistance — and its edges can be polished to optical quality, which PETG cannot match. Acrylic is also more UV-stable outdoors. For display work, optical applications, and signage where maximum clarity and scratch resistance are the primary requirements, acrylic remains the stronger specification.

Polycarbonate leads on impact resistance by a substantial margin — roughly 30 times tougher than acrylic and significantly stronger than PETG in high-energy impact scenarios. For machine guards, safety glazing, and applications where the material may be struck with significant force, polycarbonate is the correct specification. PETG sits between these two: better impact resistance than acrylic without polycarbonate's full cost premium, better surface quality than polycarbonate, and easier to fabricate and form than both. For applications where appearance and moderate impact resistance are both required and budget efficiency matters, PETG is frequently the practical answer.

PETG's combination of clarity, toughness, and ease of fabrication makes it a practical specification across retail, medical, food service, and industrial applications where acrylic's brittleness or polycarbonate's cost are drawbacks. What Should You Specify When Ordering PETG?

PETG sheet is available in clear, white, black, and colored grades. Clear PETG is the standard specification for display, optical, and inspection window applications. Colored and opaque grades are used for enclosures, signage backgrounds, and structural components where appearance rather than transparency is the primary requirement. Thickness selection should be based on the structural requirements of the application — PETG at equivalent thickness to acrylic will deflect more under load due to its lower flexural modulus, so thickness may need to be increased to achieve equivalent rigidity.

For fabricated parts, buyers should specify finished dimensions, any thermoforming requirements, and edge finish. PETG edges can be routed, sawed, and machined to clean finishes, but do not flame-polish to optical clarity the way acrylic does. Frequently Asked Questions

Is PETG clearer than acrylic?

No — acrylic transmits up to 92% of visible light compared to approximately 90% for PETG, and acrylic has a harder surface that polishes to better optical clarity. For maximum optical performance, acrylic is the stronger specification. PETG offers comparable clarity for most practical display and glazing applications while providing better impact resistance than acrylic.

How does PETG compare to polycarbonate for impact resistance?

Polycarbonate is significantly more impact-resistant than PETG — roughly 30 times tougher than acrylic and considerably stronger than PETG in high-energy impact scenarios. For machine guards, safety glazing, and applications where the material may be struck with significant force, polycarbonate is the correct specification. PETG is appropriate for moderate impact resistance requirements where polycarbonate's full performance and cost are not justified.

Is PETG safe for food contact?

Yes — PETG meets FDA requirements for food contact in many applications and is widely used in food service display cases, sneeze guards, and food processing equipment covers. Always confirm compliance for the specific application and cleaning chemicals involved, as FDA compliance depends on the specific grade and end-use conditions.

Can PETG be used outdoors?

Standard PETG has limited UV stability and will yellow and become brittle over time under prolonged direct sunlight. For outdoor applications requiring optical clarity and weatherability, acrylic is typically the better specification due to its inherent UV resistance. UV-stabilized PETG grades are available but are not as widely stocked as UV-stable acrylic.
